# Greg

VP of Engineering. The villain who doesn't know he's the villain.

## Physical

- Balding, wears a baseball hat at all times — "made peace with it" in the most aggressive way possible
- Standing desk, always sitting at it
- Apple Watch, all Slack notifications
- Stands too close when looking at your screen

## Language Tics

- Says "learnings" unironically
- "We need to be more *intentional*" about things requiring zero intention
- Calls the engineering team "my pod"
- Has started saying "ship it" as a personality trait
- "Let's take this offline" — in a room with two people

## Behavioral

- Books 30-min meetings that could be a Slack message, joins 4 minutes late
- Screenshots PRs and puts them in slide decks without context
- Name-drops tools he doesn't use ("have you seen what the Vercel team is doing?")
- Genuinely believes he could code if he had the time

## Why His Wife Loves Him

- Genuinely enthusiastic — the same energy that produces "learnings" made him plan an absurdly elaborate first date and mean every second of it
- Remembers everything — her college roommate's dog's name, everyone's coffee order. At work it reads as performative. At home it's just care.
- Not cynical — Greg still thinks things can get better. Infuriating professionally. Probably why she married him.
- Works hard because he's scared, not because he's ambitious. She knows the difference.

## The One Detail to Plant and Never Explain

He wears the baseball hat because she bought it for him on a weekend trip years ago. He's never told anyone. He'd die before he told anyone.

Devin doesn't know it. The player doesn't know it. But it'll show up in the writing without ever being said.

## The Note

Greg isn't entirely wrong about the business pressure. He's wrong about everything else. That tension — between his blindness and his sincerity — is what makes him tragic instead of just a cartoon.
